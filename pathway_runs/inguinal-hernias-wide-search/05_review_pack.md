# Inguinal hernias (adult) – wide-search review pack

## Overview
Adult primary-care pathway focused on **clear urgency thresholds** and safe use of **watchful waiting**.

## Key thresholds
- **Emergency referral (same day):** suspected strangulation or bowel obstruction (e.g. severe persistent pain, tender irreducible hernia, vomiting/obstructive symptoms).
- **Urgent referral:**
  - **all suspected groin hernias in women**
  - irreducible or partially reducible groin hernia.
- **Routine referral:** symptomatic reducible inguinal hernia (typically men), or diagnostic uncertainty.
- **Primary care watchful waiting:** minimally symptomatic/asymptomatic reducible inguinal hernia (usually men), with safety-net advice.

## Initial assessment in primary care
1. Confirm likely groin hernia clinically and assess reducibility/risk features.
2. Exclude immediate emergency presentation.
3. Record symptom burden and functional impact.
4. Document shared decision-making discussion (watchful waiting vs referral).

## Imaging position
- **Do not request routine ultrasound** for clinically obvious uncomplicated inguinal hernia.
- Use imaging selectively when diagnosis is uncertain or another diagnosis is suspected.

## Primary care management (selected non-urgent patients)
- Analgesia and activity advice.
- For patients not fit for / declining surgery: conservative management can include truss/support option.
- Safety-net: urgent review if pain escalates, hernia becomes irreducible, or obstructive features develop.

## Referral payload
- Site/side, reducibility, symptom pattern, and functional impact.
- Any episodes suggesting incarceration/obstruction.
- Sex and pregnancy context where relevant.
- Comorbidities/frailty and patient preference for/against surgery.

## Evidence confidence
**Moderate confidence.**
- Strong cross-source agreement on triage thresholds (emergency features, urgent referral in women, watchful waiting for selected minimally symptomatic cases).
