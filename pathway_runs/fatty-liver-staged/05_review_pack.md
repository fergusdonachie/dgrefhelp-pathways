# Fatty liver disease (MASLD/NAFLD)

## Page contents (Background)

- MASLD/NAFLD is common and usually linked with obesity, type 2 diabetes, and metabolic syndrome.
- Most patients have simple steatosis and can be managed in primary care.
- The key triage decision is whether there is evidence of advanced fibrosis or diagnostic uncertainty.

## Accordion: Assessment

- Take alcohol history and assess metabolic risk profile.
- Use non-invasive fibrosis risk stratification (local pathways commonly use FIB-4).
- Do not use routine LFTs alone to rule out NAFLD or advanced fibrosis.
- NICE pathway: consider ELF; ELF >=10.51 indicates advanced fibrosis.
- Additional concern features: low platelets, imaging suggestive of cirrhosis, other fibrosis-risk markers.

## Accordion: Primary care management

- Offer structured weight loss support, physical activity, and dietary advice.
- Manage diabetes, hypertension, and dyslipidaemia to target.
- Continue statins unless clear stopping criteria occur after initiation.
- Advise alcohol intake within recommended limits.
- If low fibrosis risk, continue in primary care with planned reassessment as per local protocol.

## Accordion: Who to refer

- Urgent suspicion of cancer (USoC)
  - Refer via local USoC pathway if hepatobiliary cancer concern exists.

- Urgent
  - Acute liver decompensation or severe deterioration (acute pathway).

- Routine
  - Diagnostic uncertainty.
  - Suspected advanced fibrosis (non-invasive score above local threshold and/or concerning supporting features).
  - Concern for cirrhosis or alternative liver disease.

- Include in referral
  - Alcohol history and metabolic profile.
  - Serial LFT/FBC/platelets.
  - Fibrosis score(s) and date.
  - Ultrasound and liver screen results.
  - Medicines and key comorbidities.

## Accordion: Who not to refer

- MASLD/NAFLD without significant fibrosis and no diagnostic uncertainty.
- Continue primary care management with planned restaging.
