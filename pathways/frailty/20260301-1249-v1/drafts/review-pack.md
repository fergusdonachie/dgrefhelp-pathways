# Frailty in adults — clinician review pack (detailed)

## Management principle
Frailty should be identified proactively to reduce avoidable harm from minor stressors, reduce treatment burden, and coordinate person-centred care around what matters most to the individual.

## Scope and pathway intent
- Primary care and community pathway for adults (predominantly older adults) living with frailty.
- Emphasis on practical assessment, medication/risk review, falls prevention, functional support, and clear referral boundaries.
- Frailty is treated as a dynamic state (can improve or worsen), not a fixed label.

## Case finding and assessment

### Who to consider for frailty-focused review
Offer an approach that takes account of multimorbidity/frailty where people:
- struggle with treatment workload or day-to-day function,
- have frequent urgent/unplanned care use,
- have falls risk or recurrent falls,
- are on high medicine burden (especially 10+ medicines; strongly for 15+).

### Practical frailty assessment in primary/community care
Use one practical method consistently (and combine with clinical judgement):
- gait speed impression or measured gait speed,
- PRISMA-7,
- self-rated health score.

Assessment should include:
- baseline function (mobility, ADLs, cognition, continence, nutrition, social support),
- recent deterioration triggers (infection, medication change, falls),
- current disease burden and treatment burden,
- goals, preferences and advance planning priorities.

## Detailed primary care management

### 1) Build an individualised management plan
Agree and document:
- what matters most to the patient (independence, symptom control, staying at home, etc.),
- current risks (falls, delirium risk, decompensation risk, carer strain),
- treatment priorities and de-prioritisation of low-value interventions,
- named coordinator and review interval.

### 2) Medication optimisation and treatment-burden reduction
At each structured review:
- reconcile all medicines (including OTC/herbal products),
- identify high-risk combinations and adverse-effect burden,
- consider deprescribing where benefit is limited vs burden/harms,
- reduce regimen complexity where possible,
- plan close follow-up after medication changes.

### 3) Falls and mobility prevention bundle
- assess falls history and fear of falling,
- review postural BP and medication contributors,
- address strength/balance and activity support,
- consider vision/footwear/home hazards,
- integrate with local falls pathway and community therapy services.

### 4) Nutrition, hydration, and sarcopenia focus
- screen for weight loss/reduced intake,
- optimise protein-calorie intake and hydration,
- support resistance/activity interventions where feasible,
- involve dietetic/community teams when intake is inadequate.

### 5) Cognitive and mental health review
- check for delirium vulnerability, cognitive decline, depression/anxiety,
- identify reversible contributors (pain, infection, sleep disruption, medications),
- involve carers and ensure communication plans are clear.

### 6) Monitoring cadence and safety-netting
- increase review frequency after acute events or treatment changes,
- provide explicit red-flag advice to patient/carers,
- document who to contact for urgent deterioration,
- perform at least annual comprehensive frailty-focused review when stable.

## Who to refer

### Urgent/Same-day referral
- suspected acute delirium, sepsis, major injury/fracture, or rapid functional collapse,
- acute inability to maintain safe oral intake/hydration,
- safeguarding concern or immediate carer breakdown with unsafe home situation.

### Routine specialist/MDT referral
- recurrent falls, progressive frailty with functional decline, or repeated unplanned admissions,
- complex polypharmacy where deprescribing/treatment prioritisation needs specialist input,
- diagnostic uncertainty (for example overlapping frailty, dementia, movement disorder, or reversible causes not clarified),
- need for comprehensive geriatric assessment (CGA) or coordinated multidisciplinary plan.

## Who not to refer
- stable mild frailty with manageable needs, no red flags, and good response to a primary-care-led management plan.
- continue proactive community management and time-bounded review/escalation criteria.
