# Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) in adults

## Page contents (Background)
CKD is common, usually long-term, and often managed safely in primary care. The main goals are to slow progression, reduce cardiovascular risk, and identify the smaller group needing specialist renal input.

Patients are often asymptomatic early and are identified through routine bloods (eGFR) and urine testing (ACR), especially in people with diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular disease, or older age.

## Accordion: Assessment
Confirm CKD and stage it correctly.

- Confirm persistence for at least 3 months (repeat eGFR/ACR as needed).
- Classify using both:
  - G-stage (eGFR), and
  - A-stage (ACR).
- Baseline assessment:
  - BP, cardiovascular risk, diabetes status, medications (including nephrotoxins), fluid status.
  - Blood tests: U&E/creatinine/eGFR, bicarbonate, Hb, HbA1c if relevant, lipids, calcium/phosphate as indicated.
  - Urine: ACR (preferred), urine dip for blood/protein.
- Look for red flags requiring urgent specialist review:
  - rapid fall in kidney function,
  - suspected nephritic syndrome/vasculitis,
  - severe or refractory hyperkalaemia,
  - acute-on-chronic deterioration with systemic illness.
- In CKD G3a-G5, calculate KFRE (5-year risk) to support referral decisions.

## Accordion: Primary care management
### Core treatment for most stable CKD
- Control blood pressure to guideline target and optimise renin-angiotensin system blockade where indicated.
- Use SGLT2 inhibitor therapy when eligible under current guidance.
- Prescribe statin therapy for cardiovascular risk reduction unless contraindicated.
- Optimise diabetes care and smoking cessation.
- Avoid nephrotoxins where possible (especially NSAIDs) and review medicines for dose adjustment by renal function.

### Monitoring
- Monitor eGFR and ACR at intervals based on CKD stage/risk (more frequent in advanced or progressive disease).
- Use trends, not isolated values, to assess progression.
- Recheck potassium and renal function after ACEI/ARB dose changes.

### What to do with key thresholds
- ACR >=70 mg/mmol: refer (unless already under appropriate specialist care pathway).
- KFRE 5-year risk >5% (CKD G3a-G5): refer for renal specialist assessment.
- Persistent progression despite optimisation: refer.

## Accordion: Who to refer
### Hospital admission (same day)
- Suspected rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis/vasculitis with systemic features.
- Severe hyperkalaemia or acute deterioration needing immediate specialist management.
- Acute-on-chronic kidney deterioration with clinical instability.

### Urgent suspicion of cancer (USOC)
- Haematuria meeting urology cancer pathway criteria (use local USOC route; renal referral may still be needed depending on context).

### Urgent (non-cancer outpatient)
- Rapid sustained decline in renal function.
- Heavy proteinuria with concerning progression.
- Suspected multisystem renal disease.

### Routine (outpatient)
- KFRE 5-year risk >5% (CKD G3a-G5).
- ACR >=70 mg/mmol.
- Progressive CKD despite optimal primary care treatment.
- Resistant hypertension with CKD or diagnostic uncertainty.

### Include in referral
- eGFR and ACR trend (with dates).
- BP trend and current antihypertensive/renoprotective treatment.
- Relevant blood/urine results and imaging.
- Comorbidity profile and medication list.

## Accordion: Who not to refer
- Stable CKD with low progression risk, controlled BP, and no high-risk features can remain in primary care with structured monitoring.
