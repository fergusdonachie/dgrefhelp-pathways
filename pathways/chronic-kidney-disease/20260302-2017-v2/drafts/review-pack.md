# Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) in adults

## Page contents (Background)
CKD is common and is usually managed in primary care. Most patients are identified on routine blood and urine tests before they have symptoms.

The practical aim is simple:
- confirm true CKD (not temporary kidney change),
- reduce kidney and cardiovascular risk,
- identify the smaller group who need renal specialist care.

Key terms used in this pathway:
- **G-stage** = kidney function stage by eGFR (G1 to G5).
- **A-stage** = albuminuria stage by urine ACR (A1 to A3).
- **KFRE** = Kidney Failure Risk Equation (predicts risk of needing kidney replacement therapy; used in CKD G3a-G5).
- **RAAS blockade** = ACE inhibitor or ARB treatment to reduce proteinuria and slow CKD progression.

## Accordion: Assessment
### Step 1: Confirm CKD
- CKD requires abnormal kidney markers for **at least 3 months**.
- Repeat eGFR and urine ACR to confirm persistence.

### Step 2: Stage CKD
- G-stage by eGFR:
  - G1 >=90, G2 60-89, G3a 45-59, G3b 30-44, G4 15-29, G5 <15.
- A-stage by ACR:
  - A1 <3 mg/mmol, A2 3-30 mg/mmol, A3 >30 mg/mmol.

### Step 3: Baseline checks
- BP, medication review (including nephrotoxins/NSAIDs), cardiovascular risk, diabetes status.
- Bloods: U&E/creatinine/eGFR, bicarbonate, FBC, HbA1c (if relevant), lipids.
- Urine: ACR and dipstick blood/protein.
- Arrange renal USS when indicated (for example suspicion of structural disease/obstruction).

### Step 4: Check for urgent red flags
- rapid sustained fall in kidney function,
- suspected nephritic syndrome/vasculitis,
- severe or refractory hyperkalaemia,
- acute-on-chronic deterioration with clinical instability.

## Accordion: Primary care management
### What to do for all CKD stages
- Optimise BP and cardiovascular risk.
- Start atorvastatin (usually 20 mg) unless contraindicated.
- Stop/avoid nephrotoxins where possible (especially NSAIDs).
- Adjust medicine doses to renal function.
- Give sick-day advice for medicines that worsen AKI risk during dehydrating illness.

### RAAS blockade (ACEI/ARB): when to use
- Use ACEI/ARB in CKD with albuminuria/proteinuria (especially ACR >3 mg/mmol in diabetes).
- Titrate to maximum tolerated dose.
- Recheck creatinine/eGFR and potassium after starting or dose increase.

### SGLT2 inhibitor: when to use
- **CKD + diabetes:** use dapagliflozin as part of renoprotective treatment (per local/NICE pathway).
- **CKD without diabetes:** consider dapagliflozin 10 mg daily when **ACR >22 mg/mmol**, usually as add-on to ACEI/ARB (if tolerated), and if not contraindicated by local criteria.

### Stage-based follow-up in primary care
- **G1-G2 with A1:** annual review if stable.
- **G1-G2 with A2/A3:** at least annual review; more frequent if progression.
- **G3a-G3b (non-diabetic local model):**
  - if ACR <=30 mg/mmol: annual review,
  - if ACR >30 mg/mmol: 6-monthly review.
- **G4-G5:** usually shared care / specialist-led with closer monitoring.

At each review: BP, eGFR trend, ACR trend, potassium, medication tolerance, cardiovascular risk, and progression rate.

## Accordion: Who to refer
### Hospital admission (same day)
- suspected rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis/vasculitis,
- severe hyperkalaemia or unstable acute-on-chronic deterioration,
- clinically unwell patient with acute renal decline.

### Urgent suspicion of cancer (USOC)
- haematuria meeting urology cancer criteria (use local USOC route).

### Urgent (non-cancer outpatient)
- rapid sustained eGFR decline,
- heavy/progressive proteinuria with concern,
- suspected multisystem renal disease.

### Routine (outpatient)
- **KFRE 5-year risk >5%** (CKD G3a-G5),
- **ACR >=70 mg/mmol**,
- progressive CKD despite optimal treatment,
- resistant hypertension with CKD,
- diagnostic uncertainty.

### Include in referral
- eGFR and ACR trends with dates,
- BP trend and current treatment,
- potassium and relevant blood/urine results,
- medication list and comorbidities.

## Accordion: Who not to refer
- Stable, low-risk CKD with controlled BP and no progression/high-risk features can remain in primary care with structured monitoring.

# Evidence review pack: Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) in adults

## Source-by-source summary

### 1) NICE NG203: Chronic kidney disease: assessment and management
URL: https://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/ng203

**Population/setting**
- Adults with or at risk of CKD across primary and secondary care.

**Key actionable thresholds/criteria**
- Confirm CKD by persistence of abnormal kidney function/markers for at least 3 months.
- Stage with both eGFR (G-stage) and albuminuria (A-stage).
- Use urine ACR for proteinuria assessment.
- Use KFRE in CKD G3a-G5 to support referral risk decisions.

**Referral/urgency implications**
- Referral based on progression risk, severity of albuminuria, and overall trajectory.
- Urgent escalation for rapid deterioration or concerning systemic renal presentations.

**Limitations/notes**
- NICE overview extract is high-level; threshold-level operational wording supplemented by local Right Decisions pathway.

### 2) Right Decisions NHS Dumfries & Galloway: Chronic Kidney Disease
URL: https://www.rightdecisions.scot.nhs.uk/dgrefhelp-nhs-dumfries-galloway/renal/chronic-kidney-disease/

**Population/setting**
- D&G primary care referral interface for CKD.

**Key actionable thresholds/criteria**
- Repeat eGFR promptly if newly abnormal and confirm chronicity.
- ACR thresholds used to stratify risk and follow-up.
- Local practical medication detail includes:
  - atorvastatin as default CV prevention approach,
  - ACEI/ARB at maximum tolerated dose where albuminuria/proteinuria present,
  - dapagliflozin use in diabetic CKD and selected non-diabetic CKD with higher ACR.
- CKD3 follow-up cadence example:
  - ACR <=30: annual,
  - ACR >30: 6-monthly.

**Referral/urgency implications**
- Use KFRE to quantify progression risk and support referral discussions.
- Refer for high-risk proteinuria, progression, or concerning systemic features.
- Same-day escalation for unstable/acute severe presentations.

**Limitations/notes**
- Some extracted page formatting is imperfect; key thresholds and management direction are still clear.
- Final publication wording should keep local D&G service routes explicit.

## Cross-source themes

- Strong agreement that most stable CKD is managed in primary care with structured monitoring.
- Strong agreement that CKD staging must combine kidney function and albuminuria (G + A framework).
- Strong agreement on proactive cardiovascular-risk reduction and renoprotective prescribing.
- Local D&G source provides the most practically useful threshold-level operational detail for follow-up cadence and medication positioning.
- Referral logic consistently depends on progression, proteinuria severity, and high-risk clinical features; KFRE is a useful risk communication/referral support tool in G3a-G5.

## How themes inform pathway background

The background section is written to orient GPs quickly: CKD is common, often asymptomatic, and mainly managed in primary care. It explicitly frames the core workflow as confirm chronicity, stage risk, treat progression/CV risk, and escalate high-risk patients. This reflects cross-source agreement and aligns the background directly to the practical actions in assessment/management sections.
