# Peripheral Vascular Disease / Arterial Insufficiency

## Background (page contents)

Peripheral arterial disease (PAD) affects around 20% of people over 60, though most are asymptomatic. It is caused by atherosclerosis reducing arterial blood flow to the lower limbs. Risk factors include smoking (strongest modifiable risk), diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, and chronic kidney disease.

PAD presents across a clinical spectrum:
- **Asymptomatic** — incidental finding (low ABPI, absent pulses)
- **Intermittent claudication (IC)** — reproducible muscle pain (calf, thigh, or buttock) brought on by walking and relieved by rest; prognosis is generally good, with only 1–2% per year progressing to critical ischaemia
- **Chronic limb-threatening ischaemia (CLTI)** — rest pain, non-healing wounds, or gangrene; high risk of amputation and cardiovascular death
- **Acute limb ischaemia** — a vascular emergency requiring immediate discussion with vascular surgery

PAD is also a strong marker of systemic cardiovascular risk — patients have significantly elevated risk of MI and stroke.

What must not be missed: acute limb ischaemia (the 6 Ps: pain, pallor, pulselessness, paraesthesia, paralysis, perishingly cold) and CLTI (rest pain, non-healing wounds below the knee, gangrene).

## Primary care assessment

**History**
- Claudication: calf/thigh/buttock pain, onset with walking, relieved by rest within minutes
- Rest pain: constant foot or toe pain, typically worse at night, often relieved by hanging leg dependent (over bed edge)
- Wound healing: any non-healing wounds below the knee >2 weeks
- Risk factor history: smoking, diabetes, hypertension, hypercholesterolaemia, CKD, cardiovascular disease
- Functional impact: walking distance, quality of life

**Examination**
- Colour and temperature of feet
- Peripheral pulses: femoral, popliteal, dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial
- Capillary refill time
- Trophic changes: skin atrophy, hair loss, dystrophic nails, ulceration, gangrene
- Abdominal palpation for aneurysm (AAA)

**ABPI measurement**
- Measure in all patients with suspected PAD
- Normal: 0.9–1.3
- Claudication: typically 0.5–0.9 — refer if <0.9 (D&G threshold)
- CLTI: typically <0.5 — urgent referral
- >1.3 (calcified, non-compressible arteries): unreliable result — particularly in diabetes, end-stage renal disease, and the very elderly; refer if symptomatic
- Red flag: ABPI <0.5 → discuss urgently with vascular

**Investigations**
- FBC, U&E, lipid profile, HbA1c, fasting glucose
- ECG (cardiovascular risk assessment)
- Urine dipstick (renal disease, diabetes)

**Red flags — requiring urgent action**
- Acute limb ischaemia: sudden onset severe limb pain, pallor, pulselessness, paraesthesia, paralysis, cold limb → 999 / emergency department
- Rest pain with or without tissue loss → urgent vascular referral
- Non-healing wound >2 weeks below the knee, or gangrene → urgent vascular referral
- Clinical evidence of severe infection/sepsis with systemic signs → discuss urgently with Hairmyres on-call vascular

## Primary care management

Manage all patients with intermittent claudication in primary care first. Refer only if first-line treatment fails or referral criteria are met.

**Lifestyle and risk factor modification**
- Smoking cessation: strongest intervention; cessation advice and pharmacotherapy (varenicline, NRT)
- Supervised exercise programme: 2 hours/week for minimum 3 months before referral for claudication (NICE NG237)
- Encourage walking to the point of claudication pain
- Weight reduction
- Blood pressure control: target <130/80 mmHg

**Antiplatelet therapy**
- Clopidogrel 75mg daily (preferred in PAD — CAPRIE trial; D&G local guidance)

**Lipid-lowering therapy**
- Atorvastatin 80mg daily for patients with PAD and total cholesterol >3.5 mmol/L

**Glycaemic control**
- Optimise HbA1c; diabetic patients are at higher risk of arterial calcification and CLTI

**Vasoactive drugs**
- Consider naftidrofuryl oxalate 100mg three times daily, increasing to 200mg three times daily after 3 months if tolerated; stop if no benefit after a further 3 months (NICE TA223)

**Foot care**
- Inspect feet regularly, especially in diabetic patients
- Refer for structured diabetic foot care if ulceration, deformity, or sensory neuropathy

## Who to refer

### Hospital admission (same day) — discuss urgently with Hairmyres on-call vascular / send to Emergency Department
- Acute limb ischaemia (any of: sudden pain, pallor, pulselessness, paraesthesia, paralysis, cold limb)
- Clinical evidence of severe infection/sepsis with systemic signs

### Urgent (non-cancer outpatient)
- Rest pain: constant foot or toe pain, typically worse at night and relieved by dependence
- Non-healing wound >2 weeks below the knee
- Gangrene on the foot

### Routine (outpatient)
- Quality of life severely affected by claudication, or claudication distance <40 metres (D&G threshold)
- ABPI <0.9
- ABPI >1.4 in diabetic, end-stage renal disease, or extremely elderly — result unreliable; refer if symptomatic
- Failed first-line treatment (supervised exercise, risk factor modification, naftidrofuryl)

Note: before referring, consider whether the patient would be suitable for vascular intervention which might end in surgery. If unsure, contact the vascular service first: dg.vascularservice@nhs.scot

### Include in referral
- ABPI result with date
- Baseline bloods: lipids, HbA1c, FBC, U&E
- Current medications (especially antiplatelet/anticoagulant/statin)
- Cardiovascular history and comorbidities
- Smoking status and pack-year history

## Who not to refer

- Mild/moderate claudication with ABPI 0.9–1.3, good quality of life, and no red flag features — manage in primary care
- Until first-line treatment has been tried (minimum 3 months: exercise, smoking cessation, risk factor optimisation, clopidogrel, statin, naftidrofuryl if tolerated)
- Very frail patients or those with significant comorbidities where vascular intervention would not be appropriate — discuss informally with the vascular team by email before referring: dg.vascularservice@nhs.scot
- Venous leg ulceration — this is not a vascular (arterial) referral; ensure ABPI ≥0.8 before applying compression bandaging

## Evidence review

### Source-by-source summary

**NHS D&G Arterial Insufficiency Referral Criteria (local)**
Covers routine and urgent referral thresholds specific to D&G/Hairmyres vascular service. Key thresholds: ABPI <0.9 = routine referral; claudication distance <40m = routine referral; rest pain/non-healing wound/gangrene = urgent; acute limb ischaemia/severe sepsis = same-day. Contact: dg.vascularservice@nhs.scot. Explicitly notes ABPI >1.4 unreliable in diabetes/renal/elderly.

**SIGN Guideline 89**
Scotland-specific guidance on PAD diagnosis and management. Supports supervised exercise, antiplatelet therapy (clopidogrel preferred), statin use. Aligns with D&G local criteria.

**NICE NG237**
National England/Wales guidance. Recommends supervised exercise programme (2h/week, 3 months) as first-line for intermittent claudication before intervention. Supports clopidogrel, high-intensity statin. ABPI <0.9 as diagnostic threshold consistent with D&G local criteria.

**NICE TA223**
Supports naftidrofuryl as an option for claudication. 100–200mg TID; stop if no benefit at 3–6 months. D&G local document aligns.

### Cross-source themes
- All sources agree: intermittent claudication = primary care management first; referral only if failed treatment
- Consistent ABPI thresholds: <0.9 = PAD, <0.5 = severe
- Clopidogrel preferred over aspirin for PAD (CAPRIE data)
- Atorvastatin 80mg is the high-intensity statin of choice
- Exercise therapy has strong evidence and should precede intervention
