Peripheral arterial disease (PAD) affects around 20% of people over 60, though most are asymptomatic. It is caused by atherosclerosis reducing arterial blood flow to the lower limbs. Risk factors include smoking (strongest modifiable risk), diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, and chronic kidney disease.

PAD presents across a clinical spectrum:

  • Asymptomatic — incidental finding (low ABPI, absent pulses)
  • Intermittent claudication (IC) — reproducible muscle pain (calf, thigh, or buttock) brought on by walking and relieved by rest; prognosis is generally good, with only 1–2% per year progressing to critical ischaemia
  • Chronic limb-threatening ischaemia (CLTI) — rest pain, non-healing wounds, or gangrene; high risk of amputation and cardiovascular death
  • Acute limb ischaemia — a vascular emergency requiring immediate discussion with vascular surgery

PAD is also a strong marker of systemic cardiovascular risk — patients have significantly elevated risk of MI and stroke.

What must not be missed: acute limb ischaemia (the 6 Ps: pain, pallor, pulselessness, paraesthesia, paralysis, perishingly cold) and CLTI (rest pain, non-healing wounds below the knee, gangrene).
