# Abnormal liver function tests (LFTs) in adults — evidence review summary (v2, expanded practical pathway)

## Scope
- Adults in primary care with asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic abnormal LFTs.
- Focus on practical pattern-based interpretation, repeat-testing strategy, and referral thresholds.
- Excludes acute liver failure management and pregnancy-specific pathways.

## What is different in v2
- Includes **full PDF fallback extraction** from BSG and NENC guidance (instead of link-only corroboration).
- Expands assessment and management into a **pattern-based GP workflow**.
- Incorporates practical elements from **NHS Borders RDS** and **NHS Lothian RefHelp**.

## Core consensus across sources
1. Always interpret LFTs in context (symptoms/signs, risk factors, trend, and synthetic markers).
2. Urgency is driven by decompensation/synthetic failure and red flags, not just isolated enzyme level.
3. Persistent abnormalities require structured investigation and fibrosis-risk stratification.
4. Most mild asymptomatic abnormalities are alcohol-related liver disease, MASLD/NAFLD, or viral hepatitis, but treatable less-common causes must be considered.

## Practical assessment framework for GPs

### Step 1: Immediate risk check (same day if present)
- Jaundice with concern for synthetic failure (raised INR/PT, low albumin),
- Ascites, encephalopathy, bleeding, severe systemic illness,
- Marked derangement or rapidly worsening biochemistry,
- Concerning weight loss/cholestatic picture suggesting obstruction/malignancy.

### Step 2: Confirm persistence and pattern
- Repeat abnormal LFTs when clinically appropriate and compare trend.
- Classify dominant pattern:
  - hepatocellular (ALT/AST dominant),
  - cholestatic (ALP ± GGT dominant),
  - isolated bilirubin rise,
  - mixed pattern.

### Step 3: Focused history and examination
- Alcohol, prescribed/OTC/herbal drugs, viral risk factors, metabolic syndrome features,
- Autoimmune and family history clues,
- Examination for chronic liver disease signs and hepatosplenomegaly,
- Record BMI and cardiometabolic profile.

### Step 4: First-line investigation bundle (adapt to local pathways)
- FBC, U&E, albumin, INR/PT where indicated,
- AST, ALT, ALP, GGT, bilirubin,
- Viral serology and autoimmune/metabolic tests where suggested by pattern,
- Ultrasound liver/biliary tree when indicated,
- Fibrosis triage (e.g. FIB-4 and/or local fibrosis marker pathway) in probable steatotic/alcohol-related disease.

## Pattern-based primary care management (expanded)

### 1) Predominantly ALT/AST elevation (hepatocellular pattern)
Likely common causes in primary care: steatotic liver disease, alcohol, viral hepatitis, drug-induced injury.

Action:
- review alcohol and medicines first,
- assess metabolic risk (T2DM, obesity, dyslipidaemia),
- perform liver screen and fibrosis risk assessment,
- refer earlier if ALT is markedly high, rising, persistent despite intervention, or accompanied by red flags/synthetic changes.

### 2) Predominantly ALP/GGT elevation (cholestatic pattern)
Action:
- confirm hepatic source (GGT support),
- assess for cholestatic symptoms and perform biliary imaging where indicated,
- if isolated ALP without GGT rise, assess non-hepatic causes (especially bone/vitamin D context),
- refer urgently for obstructive pattern with systemic concern or unresolved cholestatic abnormality.

### 3) Isolated raised GGT
Action:
- review alcohol and metabolic risk profile,
- check for medicine contributors,
- avoid reflex referral when isolated and low-risk; trend and reassess with lifestyle intervention,
- escalate if persistent with additional abnormalities or clinical concern.

### 4) Isolated bilirubin elevation
Action:
- distinguish unconjugated versus conjugated context,
- consider Gilbert syndrome in otherwise well patients with isolated unconjugated bilirubin,
- evaluate for haemolysis or hepatobiliary disease when features are atypical.

### 5) Synthetic dysfunction markers (albumin/INR/PT/platelets)
Action:
- treat as high-risk context rather than “routine abnormal LFT”,
- early specialist discussion/referral if synthetic failure or portal hypertension features are suspected.

## Management follow-up intervals (practical)
- Mild low-risk abnormalities: repeat and review after reversible-cause intervention.
- Persistently abnormal but stable without red flags: structured follow-up with fibrosis risk reassessment.
- Escalate promptly for any worsening trend, symptom evolution, or synthetic deterioration.

## Referral framework

### Urgent/same day
- synthetic failure/decompensation features,
- obstructive/cholestatic red flags,
- severe or rapidly worsening derangement.

### Routine hepatology/gastroenterology
- persistent unexplained abnormalities after structured work-up,
- fibrosis-risk concern (e.g. elevated FIB-4/high-risk non-invasive markers),
- suspected chronic liver disease needing specialist staging/management.
