# Abnormal liver function tests (LFTs) in adults — evidence review summary (detailed)

## Scope
- Setting: primary care triage and initial management for **incidentally or symptom-triggered abnormal LFTs in adults**.
- Aim: identify urgent/same-day red flags, define baseline work-up, and set referral thresholds.
- Exclusions: paediatric pathways, inpatient liver failure management, and disease-specific specialist pathways beyond first-line triage.

## Key clinical principles (cross-source)
1. **Do not interpret abnormal LFTs in isolation**: combine biochemical pattern + symptoms/signs + risk factors + trend over time.
2. **Synthetic dysfunction or decompensation signs override numeric thresholds** and require urgent secondary care assessment.
3. **Pattern recognition** (hepatocellular vs cholestatic vs mixed) should direct first-line investigations.
4. Mild abnormalities may be transient, but persistence without clear reversible cause needs structured evaluation/referral.

## Detailed evidence extraction

### A) Red flags and urgent referral triggers
From SPS:
- Urgent referral to secondary care/A&E when features suggest serious pathology, including:
  - **weight loss + jaundice/marked cholestasis** (possible malignancy),
  - **ascites, encephalopathy, bleeding** (possible decompensation),
  - **jaundice with low albumin or prolonged INR** (synthetic failure),
  - **marked derangement of LFTs**.
- Very high transaminases (ALT/AST >~1000 IU/L) are associated with major acute hepatic injury patterns and should trigger urgent specialist assessment.

Implication for pathway wording:
- Primary care pathway should prioritise a **red-flag first screen** before routine repeat testing.
- Any synthetic failure markers should be routed as same-day urgent action.

### B) Magnitude of abnormality and first response
From SPS:
- **<2× ULN**: generally repeat LFTs and continue focused assessment.
- **>2–3× ULN**: proceed to further investigation and/or specialist referral depending on pattern and context.
- If alcohol-related suspicion: encourage abstinence and reassess (e.g., repeat at ~1 month).

Implication for pathway wording:
- Include clear “mild vs significant” branch with local lab ULN references.
- Persistence after initial repeat and no obvious cause should trigger escalation.

### C) Pattern-led interpretation and adjunct tests
From SPS:
- ALT/AST rise reflects hepatocellular injury but is non-specific for aetiology.
- ALP + raised GGT supports cholestatic pattern; isolated ALP may be non-hepatic (consider vitamin D/bone causes).
- Albumin and PT/INR are synthetic function markers (albumin slower marker; PT/INR sensitive in acute failure).
- Thrombocytopenia in chronic liver disease context indicates higher risk and should be managed with specialist input.

Implication for pathway wording:
- Initial panel should include repeat LFTs plus synthetic markers and FBC/platelets.
- Explicitly separate “enzyme elevation” from “failure physiology”.

### D) Drug and alcohol context
From SPS:
- Medicines/supplements can produce mild/transient abnormalities; interpretation requires medication history.
- GGT may normalise after alcohol abstinence over weeks.

Implication:
- Include mandatory medication reconciliation and alcohol history in first consultation.
- Avoid over-referral where clear reversible driver exists and no red flags, but set safety-net timing.

## Areas of source agreement
- Need for **clinical-context interpretation**, not isolated number response.
- Priority of identifying **decompensation/synthetic failure**.
- Importance of **repeat testing + trend** for mild abnormalities.
- Need for escalation when abnormalities are **persistent or substantial**.

## Areas requiring local policy decisions
- Exact referral cut-offs and timing where no red flags but persistent derangement.
- Standardised first-line test bundle and sequence (varies by local hepatology access).
- Direct-access fibrosis imaging pathways (where available) and thresholds.

## Suggested threshold language for draft pathway (for editor review)
- “Urgent same-day referral if jaundice with synthetic dysfunction, decompensation features, or severe biochemical derangement.”
- “Repeat and reassess mild abnormalities (<2× ULN) in clinically stable patients after targeted history/exam and reversible-cause intervention.”
- “Escalate persistent abnormalities without clear cause, and investigate earlier when values exceed 2–3× ULN or clinical concern is high.”
