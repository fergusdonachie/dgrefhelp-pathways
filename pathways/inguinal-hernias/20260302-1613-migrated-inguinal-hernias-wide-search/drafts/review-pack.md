# Inguinal hernias in adults — DGRefHelp review draft (refined)

## Assessment
- Confirm likely groin hernia clinically and document side/site.
- Assess reducibility and current pain severity.
- Screen for emergency features: severe persistent pain, tender irreducible hernia, vomiting, abdominal distension/obstructive symptoms.
- Document functional impact and symptom progression.
- Consider alternative diagnoses if clinical picture is atypical.

## Primary care management
- For low-risk reducible minimally symptomatic hernia (typically men), watchful waiting is reasonable with shared decision-making.
- Offer analgesia and activity advice.
- Avoid routine ultrasound for clinically obvious uncomplicated inguinal hernia; use imaging only when diagnosis is uncertain.
- Safety-net clearly for urgent reassessment if pain escalates, reducibility is lost, or obstructive features develop.

## Who to refer
### Hospital admission (same day)
- Suspected strangulation or bowel obstruction (e.g. severe pain, tender irreducible hernia, vomiting/obstructive symptoms, systemic deterioration).

### Urgent suspicion of cancer (USOC)
- No routine USOC trigger in uncomplicated groin hernia pathway; use cancer pathway if separate concerning features exist.

### Urgent (non-cancer outpatient)
- All suspected groin hernias in women.
- Irreducible or partially reducible groin hernia without immediate emergency features.
- Rapidly progressive symptoms or recurrent painful incarceration episodes.

### Routine (outpatient)
- Symptomatic reducible inguinal hernia (commonly men).
- Diagnostic uncertainty requiring specialist review.

### Include in referral
- Side/site, reducibility, pain pattern and symptom duration.
- Any episodes suggesting incarceration/obstruction.
- Functional impact, occupation/activity context, and patient preference.
- Relevant comorbidity/frailty and operative risk context.

## Who not to refer
- Asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic reducible inguinal hernia (typically men) where patient chooses watchful waiting and has clear safety-net advice.
