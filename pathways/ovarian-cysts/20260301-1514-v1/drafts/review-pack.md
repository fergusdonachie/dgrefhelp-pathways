# Ovarian cysts — clinician review pack (detailed)

## Management principle
Most ovarian cysts are benign, but the pathway must rapidly identify women with possible torsion/rupture/sepsis or suspected ovarian malignancy, while avoiding unnecessary intervention for low-risk simple cysts.

## Initial assessment in primary care

### Clinical priorities at first contact
1. Exclude emergency complications:
   - sudden severe unilateral pelvic pain (possible torsion/rupture),
   - peritonism, haemodynamic instability, fever/sepsis features,
   - persistent vomiting or uncontrolled pain.
2. Identify cancer-risk symptom pattern:
   - persistent abdominal distension (bloating),
   - early satiety/loss of appetite,
   - pelvic/abdominal pain,
   - urinary urgency/frequency,
   - especially frequent/persistent symptoms.
3. Consider menopausal status because risk stratification differs in postmenopausal women.

### Baseline history/examination
- symptom duration, pattern and progression,
- menstrual and gynaecological history,
- family history of ovarian/breast cancer,
- abdominal/pelvic examination for mass/ascites,
- pregnancy status when relevant to differential diagnosis.

## Detailed primary care management

### 1) Cancer-risk work-up (NICE NG12 aligned)
- If examination shows ascites or a pelvic/abdominal mass (not clearly fibroids): urgent suspected cancer pathway referral.
- If symptom pattern suggests ovarian cancer: request serum CA125.
- If CA125 >=35 IU/ml: arrange pelvic/abdominal ultrasound.
- If ultrasound suggests ovarian malignancy: urgent suspected cancer pathway referral.
- If CA125 is normal and/or ultrasound non-malignant: assess for other causes and provide clear safety-netting.

### 2) Imaging-led risk framing
- Distinguish likely simple benign cysts from complex/suspicious morphology.
- Use local radiology descriptors to separate:
  - simple unilocular cysts,
  - complex/multiloculated/solid/septated lesions,
  - bilateral disease or associated ascites.
- Manage low-risk findings conservatively where appropriate, with time-bounded follow-up.

### 3) Premenopausal management focus
- Functional/simple cysts are common; many resolve spontaneously.
- If likely benign and asymptomatic/minimally symptomatic: conservative management with interval reassessment.
- Refer routine gynaecology for persistent symptoms, enlarging cysts, or uncertain imaging.
- Escalate urgently if complexity/suspicion increases or acute complications occur.

### 4) Postmenopausal management focus
- Lower threshold for specialist risk assessment than in premenopausal women.
- RCOG update (Dec 2025): unilateral, unilocular simple cysts <=3 cm in postmenopausal women generally do not require routine follow-up.
- Any complex features, rising concern, or discordant clinical/imaging pattern should prompt specialist review.

### 5) Symptom and safety management in primary care
- Provide analgesia and explicit advice on when to seek urgent care.
- Document red flags clearly (worsening pain, systemic illness, abdominal distension progression, reduced intake).
- Arrange planned review so persistent symptoms are not lost to follow-up.

## Who to refer

### Urgent same-day referral
- suspected torsion, rupture with instability, peritonitis, sepsis, or severe uncontrolled acute pain.

### Urgent suspected cancer pathway referral
- ascites and/or pelvic/abdominal mass not clearly fibroid,
- suspicious ultrasound findings for ovarian cancer,
- high clinical suspicion despite equivocal initial tests.

### Routine gynaecology referral
- persistent symptomatic cysts,
- enlarging cysts or unresolved lesions on interval imaging,
- uncertain diagnosis or management complexity.

## Who not to refer
- clearly low-risk simple cysts with reassuring clinical assessment and no concerning features, where conservative management and clear safety-netting are in place.

## Evidence notes
- NICE NG12 provides core primary-care cancer triage thresholds (symptoms, CA125, ultrasound and referral triggers).
- RCOG guidance informs benign-versus-oncology service boundary and postmenopausal follow-up pragmatics.
