# Ovarian cysts in adults

## Page contents (Background)
Most ovarian cysts are benign and settle without treatment. The important job in primary care is to pick out the small number of patients who may have:

- an acute complication (torsion, rupture, haemorrhage), or
- a cyst with higher risk of malignancy.

How patients often present:
- persistent bloating/fullness
- pelvic or lower abdominal pain
- urinary frequency/urgency
- early satiety
- acute severe unilateral pain (possible torsion/rupture)

Some cysts are found incidentally on ultrasound done for another reason.

## Accordion: Assessment
Start with three questions:
1. Is this an emergency?
2. Is there possible cancer risk?
3. Is this likely a low-risk simple cyst?

Clinical assessment:
- history of pain pattern, bloating/fullness, appetite change, urinary symptoms
- family history of ovarian/breast/colorectal cancer
- abdominal exam (mass, tenderness, guarding/peritonism, distension)
- pelvic/bimanual exam where appropriate

Investigations:
- First-line imaging: transvaginal ultrasound (or transabdominal if TVUS not possible).
- Classify cyst as simple vs complex, measure size, and note unilateral/bilateral, solid parts, septations, ascites.
- Bloods:
  - Postmenopausal: CA125 for any ovarian mass.
  - Premenopausal: CA125 for complex/suspicious masses (not routine for clearly simple cysts).

Postmenopausal risk score (RMI):
- RMI = U x M x CA125
- U (ultrasound score): 0 if no malignant features, 1 if one feature, 3 if two or more features
- M (menopausal score): 1 premenopausal, 3 postmenopausal
- CA125 = numeric blood result
- RMI >200 = higher malignancy risk (urgent cancer pathway)

## Accordion: Primary care management
### Premenopausal women
- Simple cyst <5 cm: no routine follow-up.
- Simple cyst 5-7 cm: repeat ultrasound (usually 3-6 months).
- Simple cyst >7 cm, or persistent/enlarging cyst: refer to gynaecology.
- Complex cyst: refer (urgency depends on symptoms and malignant features).

### Postmenopausal women
- Simple unilateral unilocular cyst <=3 cm: no routine follow-up.
- Simple cyst >3 to <5 cm with normal CA125 and no suspicious features: interval ultrasound follow-up.
- Cyst >5 cm, persistent cyst, symptoms, raised CA125, or suspicious morphology: refer.
- Any complex cyst: refer (urgent if malignant features/high RMI).

Safety-net for all:
- seek urgent same-day review for worsening pain, vomiting, fever, increasing abdominal distension, or collapse.

## Accordion: Who to refer
### Hospital admission (same day)
- suspected torsion
- suspected ruptured/haemorrhagic cyst with acute abdomen
- haemodynamic instability or uncontrolled acute pain

### Urgent suspicion of cancer (USOC)
- postmenopausal RMI >200
- suspicious malignant ultrasound features (solid components, papillary projections, ascites, bilateral complex mass, metastatic features)
- strong overall clinical suspicion of ovarian malignancy

### Urgent (non-cancer outpatient)
- symptomatic complex cyst not meeting USOC threshold but needing rapid specialist review
- persistent concerning symptoms with indeterminate imaging

### Routine (outpatient)
- premenopausal simple cyst >7 cm
- persistent/enlarging cysts on interval scanning
- postmenopausal cyst >5 cm or persistent cyst needing specialist review

### Include in referral
- menopausal status
- symptom timeline and red flags
- ultrasound findings (size, morphology, laterality)
- CA125 and RMI (if postmenopausal)
- prior imaging and change over time

## Accordion: Who not to refer
- Premenopausal simple cysts <5 cm in otherwise well patients.
- Postmenopausal simple unilateral unilocular cysts <=3 cm with no concerning features.
- Selected postmenopausal >3 to <5 cm simple cysts with normal CA125 and stable interval imaging.
