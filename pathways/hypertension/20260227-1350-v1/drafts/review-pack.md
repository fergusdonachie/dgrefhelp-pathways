# Hypertension in adults — clinician review pack (detailed)

## Management principle
Diagnose accurately (ABPM/HBPM confirmation), treat cardiovascular risk early, and use a structured stepwise regimen to achieve and maintain target blood pressure with active adverse-effect monitoring and adherence support.

## Diagnostic thresholds and definitions
- Suspect hypertension when clinic BP is persistently >=140/90 mmHg.
- Confirm diagnosis (unless severe/immediate concern) with:
  - ABPM daytime average >=135/85 mmHg, or
  - HBPM average >=135/85 mmHg.
- Stage definitions:
  - Stage 1: clinic 140/90 to 159/99 with ABPM/HBPM 135/85 to 149/94.
  - Stage 2: clinic >=160/100 (but <180/120) with ABPM/HBPM >=150/95.
  - Severe (stage 3): clinic systolic >=180 or diastolic >=120.

## Same-day referral / urgent escalation
Arrange same-day specialist assessment for:
- BP >=180/120 mmHg with retinal haemorrhage/papilloedema (accelerated HTN), or
- BP >=180/120 mmHg with life-threatening symptoms (e.g. new confusion, chest pain, heart failure features, acute kidney injury features), or
- Clinical suspicion of pheochromocytoma / other high-risk secondary causes with instability.

## Baseline primary care work-up (before or at treatment start)
Complete at diagnosis confirmation stage:
1. **Target organ and baseline risk panel**
   - Urine ACR and dipstick haematuria.
   - HbA1c, U&Es, creatinine/eGFR, lipids (total + HDL).
   - 12-lead ECG.
   - Fundoscopy (hypertensive retinopathy).
2. **Cardiovascular risk estimation**
   - Use formal CVD risk tool (QRISK/NICE-aligned; in Scotland interpret with ASSIGN 2.0 policy context where relevant).
3. **Secondary cause screen (history/exam-driven)**
   - Consider endocrine/renal/vascular causes where features suggestive.
4. **Measurement quality checks**
   - Both-arm measurement at diagnosis stage.
   - Assess for postural drop in older adults, diabetes, or symptomatic patients.

## Detailed primary care management

### 1) Lifestyle treatment (for all; re-offer repeatedly)
- Weight and diet optimisation; structured physical activity advice.
- Sodium reduction (with caution around potassium-containing salt substitutes in CKD/ACEi/ARB use etc.).
- Reduce excess alcohol; smoking cessation support.
- Reduce excess caffeine intake.
- Provide written action plan and follow-up date.

### 2) Who should start drug treatment
- **Treat all persistent stage 2 hypertension** (plus lifestyle).
- **Stage 1 (<80 years):** discuss/start treatment if target organ damage, CVD, renal disease, diabetes, or 10-year CVD risk >=10%.
- **Stage 1 under 60 with low 10-year risk:** consider treatment on lifetime risk/trajectory basis.
- **Age >=80 with stage 1:** consider treatment if clinic BP >150/90, considering frailty/multimorbidity.
- **Age <40:** consider specialist review for secondary causes and long-term risk/benefit assessment.

### 3) Stepwise antihypertensive prescribing framework

#### Step 1
- ACE inhibitor (or ARB if ACEi not tolerated) for:
  - age <55, or
  - type 2 diabetes (any age/family origin).
- CCB for:
  - age >=55 without T2DM, or
  - Black African/African-Caribbean family origin without T2DM (any age).
- If CCB not tolerated (e.g. oedema), use thiazide-like diuretic.
- Use thiazide-like diuretics in preference to conventional thiazides when initiating/changing.
- **Do not combine ACEi + ARB.**

#### Step 2
If uncontrolled on step 1 (after adherence and dose check):
- ACEi/ARB + CCB, or
- ACEi/ARB + thiazide-like diuretic, or
- CCB + ACEi/ARB (or ARB preferred in relevant ethnic group where per NICE recommendation).

#### Step 3
- Triple therapy at optimal tolerated doses:
  - ACEi or ARB + CCB + thiazide-like diuretic.

#### Step 4 (resistant hypertension)
Before escalation:
- confirm persistent elevation with ABPM/HBPM,
- reassess adherence,
- assess postural hypotension and secondary causes.
Then:
- Consider low-dose spironolactone if K <=4.5 mmol/L (with caution in reduced eGFR/hyperkalaemia risk), or
- Alternative additional diuretic/alpha- or beta-blocker if spironolactone not suitable.
- Seek specialist advice for persistent uncontrolled BP on optimised quadruple regimen.

### 4) Monitoring and treatment targets
- Clinic targets:
  - <140/90 if under 80.
  - <150/90 if 80 or over.
- Home/ambulatory targets are 5 mmHg lower than clinic equivalents.
- In postural hypotension/significant orthostatic drop, base target on standing BP and clinical tolerance.
- Recheck after treatment initiation/intensification (typically 4–6 weeks), then continue structured titration until controlled.
- Annual comprehensive review once stable: BP, adherence, side effects, lifestyle, renal/electrolyte monitoring as indicated.

### 5) Practical medication safety checks (primary care detail)
- ACEi/ARB: baseline and post-initiation renal function + potassium; watch for cough/AKI/hyperkalaemia.
- Thiazide-like diuretics: monitor sodium/potassium/renal function; consider gout risk and volume depletion.
- CCB: monitor ankle oedema, flushing, constipation (dihydropyridines).
- Spironolactone: frequent potassium/eGFR monitoring initially; avoid when hyperkalaemia risk is high.
- At each review, check OTC/herbal contributors and NSAID burden.

### 6) Adherence optimisation
- Explicitly check adherence before each step escalation.
- Prefer once-daily, non-proprietary prescribing where possible.
- Use HBPM-supported shared management for engagement and dose titration.

## Referral boundaries (non-urgent)
Refer/seek specialist advice for:
- Suspected secondary hypertension.
- Resistant hypertension despite optimised stepwise treatment and adherence checks.
- Younger adults (<40) needing deeper secondary-cause assessment.
- Complex multimorbidity/frailty where risk-benefit of tighter control is uncertain.
